\chapter{Introduction}\label{ch:introduction}

Composition is structural as well as sonic: motifs, repetition, and layered parts are how many composers think,
and music-theoretic accounts likewise treat tonal music as hierarchically organized rather than as a flat sequence of
events~\cite{Lerdahl_Jackendoff_1983}, yet common production tools store music as flat note events.
When a phrase repeats or varies, the piano roll makes every copy editable by hand, and the rules that generated the
pattern are rarely recoverable from the event list alone.
This thesis asks whether a small compiled language can keep that structure explicit in source form and expand it
deterministically into a Standard MIDI File, without requiring a language runtime at playback.

We present \textit{Motivo}, a domain-specific language and offline compiler for symbolic composition, together with
Motivo Studio, a browser-based studio that wraps the toolchain in a deployable product.
The C++ compiler comprises approximately 5{,}300 lines of hand-written source, excluding the generated lexer and parser,
the test suite, and third-party dependencies.
The work is scoped to offline, rule-based expansion into portable symbolic output: Motivo complements established
digital audio workstation workflows rather than replacing them.
Section~\ref{sec:use-cases} outlines four representative use cases; the evaluation in Chapter~\ref{ch:evaluation} and
the conclusions in Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion} return to those scenarios with the evidence gathered here.
